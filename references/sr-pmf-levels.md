# SR PMF Levels — Reference

The five levels of product-market fit, as used across this plugin. Every skill anchors its advice to the founder's current level.

This framework comes from Second Rodeo's work with portfolio companies. It is opinionated and direct on purpose.

---

## The core idea

PMF is not binary. It is a stack — a sequence of certainties a founder builds, in order. Each level has a specific test, specific evidence, and specific GTM work that fits it. Doing the right work at the wrong level is the single most common failure pattern in early-stage GTM.

The framework is also messier in practice than on paper. A real founder is rarely cleanly at one level — they're "high L2, sliding into L3" or "L3 in one segment, L1 in another." The job of the diagnostic is to find the most accurate level given the evidence, then route advice from there.

---

## The five levels

### Level 1 — Problem Unclear

**The test:** A specific group of people names the same problem, unprompted.

**What this looks like in real life:**
- Customer conversations bring up a recurring frustration in their own words, before the founder mentions it
- The founder can describe the problem in one or two sentences without using product language
- Multiple people from a similar context (similar role, stage, or industry) describe the same shape of pain

**What it doesn't look like:**
- Customers nodding politely when the founder describes a problem
- A problem the founder is intellectually convinced exists but can't quote anyone naming
- A problem that's real but described differently by every person who has it (that means the framing isn't there yet)

**Common failure pattern at this level:**
Founders try to scale GTM. They build a website, run paid acquisition, hire a head of marketing. None of it works because the underlying story isn't clear yet. They blame the channel.

**What the right work looks like:**
Listening. Founder-led conversations. ICP and problem discovery. Light experiments to test message-market resonance. Minimal infrastructure — just enough CRM and tracking to learn from what's happening. No big spend. No hires.

---

### Level 2 — Solution Unclear

**The test:** A defensible point of view on the *type* of solution this problem calls for.

**What this looks like:**
- The founder can describe the shape of the right solution, even if the specific feature set is still in motion
- Customer conversations validate that "yes, something like that is what I'd want"
- The team has a working hypothesis on category — "this is more X than Y" — and that framing holds up across customer conversations
- A few early users actively engage with the product (or prototype) and give specific feedback

**What it doesn't look like:**
- A long feature list that customers say "yes" to politely
- A product that does five things adequately and one thing well — that's a sign the solution shape isn't clear yet
- "We could go in any direction" — that's L1, not L2

**Common failure pattern:**
Founders build outbound machines, hire SDRs, run content programs — at the level where their job is still to prove they have the *right kind of solution*. The motion can't compound because the thing being distributed is still in flux.

**What the right work looks like:**
Targeted experiments. Content that explains the founder's POV on how this problem should be solved. A few design partner conversations. Light scaling experiments to test specific messaging — not to scale what already works, but to learn what works.

---

### Level 3 — Not Yet Inevitable

**The test:** ~40% of users would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared (Sean Ellis test). Customers describe the value in their own language without being prompted.

**What this looks like:**
- A real subset of users have integrated the product into how they work
- Churn from the right segments is low — not because of contracts, but because they'd notice if it went away
- Customers describe the product to each other in similar language, and that language sounds like the founder's
- The founder can name 5-10 specific customers who would champion the product without being asked

**What it doesn't look like:**
- High usage but low emotional attachment
- A product where every customer describes it differently because they each found their own use case
- "Everyone could use this" — that's a sign you don't yet know who genuinely needs it

**Common failure pattern:**
Founders try to broaden too early — chase larger segments, expand to adjacent markets, add features for new buyer types. The result is dilution. The 40% disappointment number drops.

**What the right work looks like:**
Narrow focus to the strongest ICPs. Concentrated distribution where pull is strongest. Stop averaging signals across weak segments. The job is to *deepen* before broadening.

---

### Level 4 — PMF Emerging

**The test:** A repeatable way to reach and serve the right users. The whole team can describe the ICP in the same words.

**What this looks like:**
- A small set of channels reliably produce qualified pipeline
- Conversion rates are stable and predictable enough to plan against
- New customers come from patterns the team can describe — not luck or one-off heroics
- The internal narrative is consistent: sales, marketing, and the founder all tell the same story
- Hiring is starting to be possible — the role exists clearly enough that someone other than the founder can do it

**What it doesn't look like:**
- A big pipeline number with inconsistent close rates
- A team that uses different language in different rooms
- Growth that depends on the founder personally being in every deal

**Common failure pattern:**
Founders treat this as the finish line and over-invest in scale before durability is proven. They hire a CRO, build out RevOps, raise a big round. Then a market shift or a single bad quarter exposes that the motion was repeatable but not yet durable.

**What the right work looks like:**
Expand distribution. Identify additional paths — partners, community, content, paid — with clear ROI and attribution. Build the systems that scale. Tighten the ICP into a written, shared instrument the whole team uses.

---

### Level 5 — Fit Is Durable

**The test:** Growth without losing coherence or trust. The narrative, ICP, and distribution all compound on each other.

**What this looks like:**
- The company can grow into adjacent ICPs without diluting positioning
- Customer trust extends across new product or segment expansions
- The motion is now a system — channels reinforce each other, signal flows back into strategy, the team stays aligned without needing the founder in every room
- Other companies start describing themselves "like [your company]" — the narrative is owned

**What it doesn't look like:**
- Growth that requires constantly resetting the story
- Expansion that confuses existing customers about what the company is
- A motion that works at scale but relies on specific people not leaving

**What the right work looks like:**
Act II / III GTM architecture. Decide whether to expand via new product, new buyer, or new segment — and in what order. Portfolio-level GTM. Strategic decisions about where to invest the compounding advantage.

---

## How to use this reference

When advising a founder, always anchor to their level. The question "how should I do X?" gets a different answer at each level.

If the level is unclear, run the PMF diagnostic. The answer to "what level am I?" is itself diagnostic — a founder who can't say is usually L1 or low L2.

If the founder is split across levels (e.g., "L3 in segment A, L1 in segment B"), advise to the *most certain* level for the work being asked about. A distribution question for segment A gets L3 advice. A narrative question that has to cover both gets L1 advice (because the broader story isn't clear yet).

The framework is directional. Don't be precious about the exact level — be precise about the work that fits.
