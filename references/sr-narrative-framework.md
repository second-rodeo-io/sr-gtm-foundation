# SR Narrative Framework — Vision to Values

A distillation of a company, organized top-down from Vision to Values. Used as the skeleton for every narrative & positioning doc this plugin produces — for Second Rodeo's own work and for any company that runs through the system.

## Where this comes from

Jeff Weiner's Vision to Values framework, developed during his time as CEO of LinkedIn, is the cleanest tool we've found for codifying a company top-down. It runs nine layers, from the most aspirational (Vision) to the most operational (Values). Each layer flows from the one above it. Applied annually, it keeps leadership teams aligned and gives the rest of the company a map of how the dream connects to Tuesday's decisions.

We use a top-down structure because it solves the problem most positioning docs have: sections that don't clearly connect to each other. Forcing the logic to be vertical means every section is downstream of the one above, and upstream of the one below.

This framework runs the nine V2V layers, then adds an operating layer underneath that turns strategy into execution.

> The original V2V sits at [nextplayventures.com/vision-to-values](http://nextplayventures.com/vision-to-values). Everything below respects Weiner's original definitions and order; what's added is the narrative-and-positioning craft that lives underneath each layer.

## How to use this

Each section has four things:

- **Purpose** — what the section is supposed to do in the operating system
- **Questions it has to answer** — the checklist. If any of these aren't answered, the section isn't finished
- **What "done" looks like** — how you know it's working
- **Where it usually goes wrong** — the traps

Do the sections in order. The top of the stack first, the bottom last. **Skipping ahead breaks the chain** — a mission written before the vision is clear ends up measurable but uninspiring; values written before culture is defined end up generic. The discipline of the order is most of the work.

## Three principles from doing this in practice

These are the three lessons that materially change how the work lands. They are upstream of every section below.

### 1. Lead with vision, not problems

The strongest instinct when writing a narrative doc is to start with the problem space — the five things wrong with the category, the broken status quo, the list of pain points. Resist that. Leading with problems puts the reader into debate mode. They start arguing with whether your problems are canonically true, whether they see themselves in each one, whether you picked the right five. You lose them before you've said anything about yourself.

Lead with the vision instead. Paint the future you're trying to create. The reader puts themselves inside it and prescribes their own problem that fits the vision you're painting. You don't have to be dogmatic about what's wrong with the world — they'll tell you what's wrong with their world once they can see what your world looks like.

Problems still belong in the document. They just don't belong at the front of it. **Problems are what you sell against in specific moments. Vision is what you sell from, always.**

### 2. Ground truth beats theoretical framing

"The industry has problem X" is a claim the reader can debate. "In our experience, we found X" is a claim they can't. Write from lived experience wherever possible — the insight you had as a consultant, the pattern you saw across companies you operated at, the moment with a specific customer that changed how you thought about the work. This does two things: it makes the document harder to argue with, and it forces specificity. An insight that came out of a real experience has texture. A generalization about the industry has none.

This is also why interviews beat document synthesis. The strongest versions of these docs come from someone speaking directly about what they've seen, captured in their voice. Feeding a stack of prior writing into an LLM and asking it to find the connective tissue produces something that sounds like it was written by committee — because in a sense it was.

### 3. This doc is both externally facing and internally governing

It's tempting to separate "how we talk about ourselves externally" (a positioning doc) from "how we operate internally" (a culture doc or playbook). Don't. The strongest narrative docs are both — the constitution that tells the company how to think about itself, **and** the source of truth for every externally facing surface. Home behavior and guest behavior should be consistent. You don't eat with your hands at home and use a fork in public. If the two drift, the brand feels hollow in market, because it's no longer rooted in how the company actually operates.

What this means in practice: the same document powers the website, the sales deck, the recruiting pitch, the onboarding materials, and the internal operating principles. Different surfaces pull different sections. The source of truth is one.

## Part I — The Dream (aspirational layers)

### 1. Vision

**Purpose.** The true north. The long-term dream the company is pulling toward. Primary job is to inspire and create a shared sense of purpose.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- What world are we trying to create? Not a product we're trying to ship.
- Is it big enough to motivate the best people for the next decade?
- Would it still be the right dream if the product changed?

**What "done" looks like.** The vision can be said in a single sentence. It survives the test of being read aloud in an all-hands without anyone rolling their eyes. It gives the team a reason to care that isn't tied to any specific feature or quarter.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Mistaking a product roadmap for a vision. Writing something so abstract it could apply to any company. Hedging the ambition. The vision should feel slightly too big — if it feels comfortable, it isn't the vision. The most common mistake: letting the problem space bleed into the vision section. The vision is the future state. What's wrong with the present state belongs later, if at all.

**Length.** One sentence. Supporting paragraph if needed.

### 2. Mission

**Purpose.** The overarching, measurable objective — typically on a 3–5 year horizon — that turns the vision into something the company can make progress against. Brief. Achievable. Ideally inspirational.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- If the vision is the dream, what is the concrete thing we're trying to accomplish in service of it?
- Can we measure whether we're getting closer?
- Can a new hire repeat it back without having to ask follow-up questions?

**Tests a strong mission passes:**
- It's brief enough to remember.
- It minimizes use of the word "and."
- It doesn't require clarifying questions when first presented.
- It's uniquely identifiable to the company — a competitor couldn't swap their logo onto it.

**What "done" looks like.** A sentence short enough to fit on a slide. Every employee can say it. It gives the leadership team a clear way to answer "are we on track."

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Missions that are either (a) restated visions (too abstract), or (b) restated roadmaps (too tactical). The mission sits between those two. If it reads the same as the vision, you've just restated the dream. If it reads the same as the quarterly plan, you've restated the work.

**Length.** One sentence.

## Part II — The Market (positioning layers)

### 3. Value Proposition

**Purpose.** The benefit the company promises to deliver to its customers. The reason anyone should use us instead of an alternative. This is where positioning work properly begins.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- What's the specific change in the world that makes this valuable now?
- What does every other company in our space believe that we think is wrong?
- What's the one word that captures what we deliver at our best? (Stripe → Infrastructure. Airbnb → Belonging. Nike → Greatness.)
- What's the functional job we do, and what's the emotional job underneath it?
- If the customer only remembers one sentence about us, what do we want it to be?

**Structure:**
- The macro shift that makes us necessary now. Written as a claim.
- What the incumbents assume that's no longer true.
- The core insight — functional and emotional.
- The promise — compressed into a single sentence.

**What "done" looks like.** A smart operator reads this and either strongly agrees or strongly disagrees — not "interesting." The core insight is specific enough that a competitor couldn't credibly claim it. The promise passes the test of being something a reasonable competitor would hesitate to say.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Starting with features instead of change. Describing a trend without picking a side. Landing on a truth that's true but unowned ("customers want simplicity" — everyone wants that, no one owns it). Skipping the emotional layer because it feels soft. And the big one: writing the value prop from theory rather than lived experience. "The industry has problem X" invites debate. "In our experience, we found X" doesn't. Write it the way you'd say it to a founder across the table, not the way a deck would say it.

**Length.** One to two pages. Prose.

### 4. Target Audiences

**Purpose.** The segment of customers whose needs we prioritize. Effective segments have unique needs tied to the value proposition, are measurable, and are focused enough to actually serve well.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- Who is the primary audience — defined by situation, not demographics?
- What job are they hiring us to do?
- What's happening in their world the moment they start looking for a solution?
- Is there a secondary audience we take care to reasonably serve?
- Who is this explicitly not for?

**Structure:**
- Primary audience — job to be done, triggering situation, consideration set.
- Secondary audience (if applicable) — and what we do differently to serve them.
- Non-fit — who this doesn't serve, and where we'd point them instead.
- Category frame — the frame of reference the primary audience will use, on our terms rather than the incumbents'.

**What "done" looks like.** A salesperson can qualify in or out on a thirty-minute call. A content writer can picture the exact moment in a customer's week when they'd land on our homepage. The non-fit is specific enough that we actually turn people away.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Demographic personas that describe the buyer's title but not their situation. Listing industries instead of triggers. Refusing to define a non-fit, which ends up protecting nobody.

**Length.** Two to three pages.

## Part III — The Plan (strategic layers)

### 5. Strategy

**Purpose.** The 1–3 year theory of how the company moves toward its 3–5 year mission, given the competitive landscape and the dynamics most heavily influencing it. Strategy lives at a horizon shorter than the mission and longer than the quarterly plan — it's the multi-year theory of how we win, not the list of tactics that flow from it (those come next, in Priorities).

**Questions it has to answer:**
- What's the competitive landscape, honestly described?
- Which dynamics — technology, macro, geopolitical, category — are moving hardest right now, and how do we position relative to them?
- What's our theory of how we win?
- What's the unique mechanism or advantage that makes our theory credible?
- What will we refuse to do because it would undermine the strategy?

**Structure:**
- Honest read of the competitive landscape.
- The dynamics shaping it (tailwinds and headwinds).
- Our theory of how we win.
- The mechanism that makes it credible.
- Strategic guardrails — what we will and won't do.

**What "done" looks like.** A new hire can read this and understand why we're doing what we're doing instead of something else. The leadership team agrees on the theory, even when they disagree on the tactics.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Confusing strategy with priorities. (A list of things we're going to do is not a strategy. A theory of how we win over the next 1–3 years is.) Pulling the horizon too short — strategy that lives inside one year is really just a priority list with extra adjectives. Pulling the horizon too long — strategy that's indistinguishable from the mission has nothing concrete the team can act on. Describing the market without taking a position on how it behaves. Writing a strategy so abstract it could apply to any competitor.

**Length.** Two to four pages.

### 6. Priorities

**Purpose.** The 12-month stack-ranked list of tactics that manifest the strategy. Strategy answers "how do we win over the next 1–3 years." Priorities answer "so what does that mean we actually do this year." First principle: start with "if we could only do one thing, what would it be?" — and don't move to item two until item one is resolved.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- If we could only do one thing over the next [quarter / year], what would it be?
- What's the second thing? The third?
- What are we consciously not doing, and why?
- Who owns each one?

**Structure:**
- A ranked list, top to bottom, with the one thing at the top.
- For each: the owner, the expected outcome, the timeframe.
- An explicit "not doing" list, so deprioritized work doesn't quietly creep back in.

**What "done" looks like.** Anyone on the team can name the top priority without hesitation. The list is short enough to remember — ideally three to five items. Each item has an owner.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Too many priorities — at which point nothing is a priority. No ranking — which leaves everything equal-weight and leaves the team unable to make tradeoffs. No "not doing" list, so scope quietly grows.

**Length.** One page.

### 7. Objectives

**Purpose.** The measurable goals aligned with mission and strategy. Fewer is better. Simpler is better. First principle: if you can't measure it, you can't fix it.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- What are the numbers that would tell us we're winning?
- Are they few enough to remember, and simple enough that everyone on the team can recite them?
- Do they actually measure what we care about — or just what's easy to measure?
- What's the cadence for reviewing them?

**Structure:**
- The top-line objectives (revenue, retention, a mission-specific metric).
- The supporting objectives that feed them.
- The review cadence — weekly, monthly, quarterly.

**What "done" looks like.** The leadership team can say the objectives from memory. The objectives actually map to the mission, not just to what's easiest to count. When the numbers move, the team knows whether that's good or bad.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Too many objectives — at which point the team is measuring instead of executing. Vanity metrics that feel good but don't map to the mission. No review cadence, so the metrics drift out of the conversation within a quarter.

**Length.** Half a page.

## Part IV — The Personality (operating layers)

### 8. Culture

**Purpose.** The company's personality — who we are, and more importantly, who we aspire to be.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- How do we actually behave, at our best?
- How does that differ from how every other company in the space behaves?
- What's the vibe of a room when this culture is working? What's the vibe when it's not?
- How does this show up in the brand — in voice, tone, visual identity, and the thousand small choices that add up to a point of view?

**Structure:**
- Cultural posture — three to five sentences describing who we are at our best.
- What this sounds like in practice (tone principles, vocabulary to use and avoid).
- What this looks like in practice (visual direction, design principles, reference points).
- Where the culture flexes — internal vs. external, sales vs. recruiting, etc.

**What "done" looks like.** A new hire can draft copy in week one that reads like it came from the company, not from a template. Two different designers produce work that looks like it came from the same place. The culture is specific enough that candidates who don't fit self-select out.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Describing culture in generic terms ("we're a fast-moving team that cares about excellence"). Defining voice before the positioning is done — at which point voice ends up carrying weight it shouldn't have to. Vocabulary principles that are too abstract to actually use.

**Length.** Two to three pages, with visual references where useful.

### 9. Values

**Purpose.** The principles that guide the organization's day-to-day decisions. A defining component of culture. These are the tiebreakers when the answer isn't obvious.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- When there's a hard call between two reasonable options, what principles do we use to decide?
- What do we believe that makes us make different choices than a competitor would in the same spot?
- Which values are non-negotiable, and which are context-dependent?
- What's the value that repels the wrong employees, customers, and partners?

**Structure:**
- Three to eight values, each:
  - Named in a phrase, not a single word.
  - Explained in one to three sentences.
  - Grounded in the behavior it's supposed to produce.
- A clear statement of what this means we won't do.

**What "done" looks like.** When two reasonable people disagree, the values resolve it. The values sound like us, not like a generic list. If swapping our name for a competitor's would make the list equally true for them, it's not our values — it's a poster.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Values that are aspirational but unactionable ("integrity," "excellence"). Too many values — anything beyond eight becomes wallpaper. Values that describe what we wish we were, not what we actually are at our best.

**Length.** One to two pages.

## Part V — The Operating Layer (where positioning becomes real)

These two sections sit underneath the nine layers above and are where narrative and positioning work translates into execution. They exist *in addition* to the core structure — not as replacements for any of it.

### 10. Narrative Pillars

**Purpose.** The three to five themes the company talks about over and over. Pillars turn the strategy into repeatable content, sales language, and product marketing without needing a new narrative every quarter.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- What are the three to five ideas we want to be known for?
- For each one: one-sentence explanation, proof, example language.
- How does each pillar map back to the value proposition?

**Structure.** For each pillar:
- The pillar name (two to four words).
- One-sentence explanation.
- Proof points — the data, the customer quote, the example.
- Example language — a headline, a tweet, a line in a sales deck.

**What "done" looks like.** A content team can write against any pillar without ambiguity. A sales rep can tell you which pillar any objection maps to. The pillars don't overlap so much that collapsing two into one would lose anything.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Too many pillars (five is the ceiling, three is often better). Pillars that sound like values instead of claims about what we do. No proof, so the pillars stay aspirational forever.

**Length.** Three to five pages.

### 11. Application

**Purpose.** Turn this from a document into an operating system. Without this section, everything above sits in a drawer.

**Questions it has to answer:**
- How does this show up on the website, in sales, in product, in hiring, in content?
- What changes as a result of this document being finalized?
- Who owns maintaining this, and when do we revisit it?

**Structure.** For each surface, what changes:
- Website — hero, key pages, copy shifts.
- Sales — deck changes, talk track, discovery questions.
- Product — onboarding language, feature naming.
- Hiring — job descriptions, interview pitch.
- Content — what we publish, against which pillars, on what cadence.
- Ownership and cadence — who owns this doc, and when it gets revisited.

**What "done" looks like.** Six weeks after the doc ships, the website, sales deck, and job descriptions all reflect it. Someone new to the company can use this section to figure out what to do.

**Where it usually goes wrong.** Stopping at the narrative and assuming execution will follow. It won't. Without this section, the doc ages out inside a quarter.

**Length.** Three to five pages.

## A note on time horizons

The layers compress as you move down the stack. Each horizon flows into the one below.

- **Vision** — ~10 year dream. The world we're trying to create.
- **Mission** — 3–5 year measurable target. The concrete thing we're trying to accomplish in service of the vision.
- **Strategy** — 1–3 year theory of how we win. The multi-year approach that moves us toward the mission.
- **Priorities** — 12-month ranked tactics. What the strategy means we actually do this year.
- **Objectives** — in-year metrics. What we measure to know whether the priorities are working.

If any layer collapses into the one above or below it, that layer isn't doing its job.

## A note on cadence

This framework is applied annually, not once. The layers tighten as the company matures. The vision might hold for a decade; the priorities shift every quarter. The discipline is reviewing each layer on its own cadence:

- **Vision, Mission** — revisited rarely. If they're changing every year, one of them isn't doing its job.
- **Value Proposition, Target Audiences, Strategy** — reviewed quarterly, updated annually or as market dynamics shift.
- **Priorities, Objectives** — reviewed quarterly.
- **Culture, Values** — codified once, reinforced continuously, revisited when the company hits an inflection point (new stage, major hire, pivot).
- **Narrative Pillars, Application** — reviewed with the Value Prop cycle. When the positioning sharpens, these update with it.

This is a living document. Update the structure here first, then propagate the change to every surface that draws from it.

## How sections work across PMF levels

The cell labels below describe the *quality* of narrative work expected at each level. They are not section gates. **At L2 and above, the entire framework is available to the founder.** A founder at L2 can draft Strategy or Values if they want — Claude should note where evidence is thin and label hypotheses as hypotheses, but should not refuse the work.

**L1 is the only gated level.** At L1, the founder hasn't validated the problem yet. Drafting Strategy, Culture, or Values at L1 puts words around something the company isn't yet — those layers need market signal underneath them. So at L1, the work is just the top of the stack: Vision and Mission. Everything below comes after the problem is clear.

| Level | Narrative cell | Sections available | What "done" looks like at this level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L1** | Hypothesis | Vision (1) and Mission (2) only | A working draft of the dream and a measurable 3–5 year objective. Internal use only. The other sections require more market signal than L1 has. |
| **L2** | Category bet | All 11 sections available | Vision and Mission held steady. Value Prop and Target Audiences are the active work. Strategy below can be drafted but will be lighter — Claude labels what's hypothesis vs. what's earned. |
| **L3** | Customer language | All 11 sections available | Sections 3–4 (Value Prop, Target Audiences) start using language customers actually use. Strategy and Priorities become more grounded. |
| **L4** | Internal alignment | All 11 sections available | The whole team can quote the doc. Sections 5–7 (Strategy, Priorities, Objectives) are load-bearing. Culture and Values get codified. |
| **L5** | Owned | All 11 sections available | The story is a strategic asset. Narrative Pillars and Application turn the doc into an operating system across every surface. |
