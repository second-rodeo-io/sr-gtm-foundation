# SR Narrative Framework — 12 Sections, Scaled by Level

Second Rodeo's canonical narrative & positioning structure. Synthesizes Andy Raskin's strategic narrative work, April Dunford's positioning framework (*Obviously Awesome*), and the manifesto tradition used by Linear, Apple, and Patagonia.

This framework is the source for the `sr-narrative-builder` skill. The full 12 sections are below. The skill scales which sections are appropriate based on the founder's PMF level.

---

## Why 12 sections (and not the 9-element ladder)

Some early SR materials describe narrative as a 9-element ladder (Vision → Mission → Value Prop → Target Audience → Strategy → Priorities → Objectives → Culture → Values). That ladder is useful as a quick check but it's not the deepest version of the work.

The 12-section framework below is the canonical one. It moves in the order a prospect's brain actually moves:

1. Something in the world has changed (gets attention without putting them on the defensive)
2. There are winners and losers (creates stakes without manufacturing fear)
3. Here is what winning looks like (Promised Land — specific, desirable, hard to reach alone)
4. Here is why reaching it is hard (where competitive alternatives live)
5. Here is what we do (features as gifts that remove obstacles, not as a list)
6. Here is who this is and isn't for (best-fit + explicit non-fit)
7. Here is the proof (evidence, not claims)
8. Here is the operational layer that keeps the story consistent across every surface

Sections 1–7 are the *narrative*. Section 8 onward is the *infrastructure* that makes the narrative executable. Both are required. A narrative without infrastructure drifts within a quarter. Infrastructure without a narrative produces consistent, on-brand content that doesn't move anyone.

## How sections work across PMF levels

The cell labels below describe the *quality* of narrative work expected at each level. They are not section gates. **At L2 and above, the entire 12-section framework is available to the founder.** A founder at L2 can draft Section 7 ("Who This Is For") if they want — Claude should note where evidence is thin and label hypotheses as hypotheses, but should not refuse the work.

| Level | Narrative cell | Sections available | What "done" looks like at this level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L1** | Hypothesis | 1, 2, 3 only | A working draft of the change in the world, the stakes, and the positioning line. Internal use only. The other sections require more market signal than L1 has. |
| **L2** | Category bet | All 12 + manifesto + key messaging modules | The narrative is a defensible POV on the category. Some sections (Proof, Who This Is For) will be lighter because evidence is still thin — Claude labels what's hypothesis vs. what's earned. |
| **L3** | Customer language | All 12 + manifesto + key messaging modules | The narrative starts using language customers actually use. Proof section becomes more grounded. Section 7 (Who It's For) gets sharper. |
| **L4** | Internal alignment | All 12 + manifesto + key messaging modules | The whole team can quote the narrative. Operational layer (voice, vocabulary, guardrails) is in place and used in real surfaces. |
| **L5** | Owned | All 12 + manifesto + key messaging modules | The story is a strategic asset. Other companies describe themselves relative to it. The category is shaped, not borrowed. |

### Why L1 is still gated

A founder at L1 has not yet validated the problem. Writing a manifesto, a Strategic Guardrails section, or a Voice & Tone document at L1 is putting words around something the company isn't yet. The manifesto in particular is *earned* by living the story, not by writing it. So at L1, Claude drafts only the first three sections — the change in the world, the stakes, and a working positioning line — and explains why the rest will be wasted work until more market signal exists.

At L2 and above, the founder has enough signal that all sections are productive work. The quality of each section will vary by level (Proof at L2 will be lighter than Proof at L4), but the framework is fully unlocked.

## The 12 sections

### Part I — The Narrative

#### 1. Positioning in One Line

The single sentence someone should be able to recall after reading this document. Everything else builds to it or flows from it.

Often paired with a one-sentence vision statement.

#### 2. The Change

The shift in the world that creates the opportunity. Not a problem statement — a statement about how the ground has moved under everyone's feet. This is what grabs attention.

Best when there are two or three changes happening simultaneously and most companies are operating as if only one of them is true.

#### 3. The Stakes

Winners and losers. Who wins in this new world, who loses, and why the status quo is no longer safe. Creates urgency without manufacturing fear.

#### 4. The Promised Land

What winning actually looks like for someone using the product. Not the product. A future state. Specific enough that the customer can picture themselves in it and know whether they want it.

#### 5. Why This Is Hard to Reach Alone

The obstacles to the Promised Land. This is where competitive alternatives live — what someone would do if the company didn't exist, and why each of those paths fails. This section earns everything said about the company in the next one.

#### 6. What We Actually Do

The solution. Not a feature list — the specific gifts that remove the obstacles above. This section only works because the previous section earned it.

#### 7. Who This Is For (and Who It Is Not)

Best-fit customers and explicit non-fit. Most positioning docs stop at "who this is for." The disciplined ones say who it's *not* for — which is how you protect the promise to the people it is for.

### Part II — The Operational Layer

#### 8. Proof

Evidence that the story can come true. The payoff to everything above.

Specific over general. "Customer X did Y in Z weeks" beats "customers see results."

#### 9. Market Category & Competitive Frame

The frame of reference the company positions inside. What the company is explicitly *not*. Stance on competitors.

#### 10. Voice & Tone

How the company sounds, at any altitude. The goal: someone reads something the company wrote and can tell within a paragraph that it's theirs.

Includes: underlying posture, what this sounds like in practice, vocabulary (use / avoid lists).

#### 11. Strategic Guardrails

Principles that should inform every product, marketing, and engagement decision. When there's a hard call, these are the tiebreaker.

### Part III — The Long Horizon

#### 12. The Long Horizon

Where this is going. How to talk about it with different audiences (investors vs. founders vs. team).

### Optional — The Manifesto

The longer emotional statement, for the website's about page, the all-hands, the recruiting pitch. Only earned at L5. Anything earlier is decoration.

### Optional — Key Messaging Modules

The modular pieces that propagate from the narrative: positioning line, tagline, vision, elevator pitch, insight, provocation, promise.

These should only be locked in at L4+, when the narrative is stable enough that consistency across surfaces matters more than experimentation.

## The hard rule: narrative before distribution

A distribution motion built on an unclear story will not compound. When a founder wants to skip narrative work and go straight to channels, the answer is no. Slow down and go back to the narrative.

This is why the orchestrator and distribution aligner skills will, at L1 and L2, insist on narrative work before tactical distribution work.

---

## Authoring principles

These are the principles the narrative builder skill uses when drafting:

**Specific over vague.** "We rebuilt the narrative and inbound demo requests doubled in eight weeks" beats "we accelerated growth."

**Earned confidence over declared ambition.** Don't announce that the company is reinventing the category. Describe what working with the product actually feels like and let the reader get there.

**Direct over clinical.** "We'll tell you what isn't working" beats "We provide candid strategic feedback."

**Calm over urgent.** No scarcity, no fear, no "book a demo before it's too late."

**Honest about what's hard.** When something is uncertain, say so. False precision is worse than honest uncertainty.

**Warm, not corporate.** A real human voice. A little dust on the boots is part of the tone.

## When using this framework with a founder

The narrative builder skill always:

1. Tells the founder which sections are appropriate for their level (and why the others would be wasted work)
2. Asks anchoring questions on the change in the world, the promised land, and who it's not for — regardless of level
3. Produces a draft with a prominent "EDIT ME HEAVILY" header
4. Reminds the founder that narrative becomes "owned" through living with it — through reading it aloud, showing it to customers, noticing what doesn't sound like them

The 30% rule: when Claude hands the founder a narrative draft, that draft is roughly 30% of the way to a real narrative. The other 70% is the founder's editing, customer testing, and rewriting. This number is approximate but the principle is firm.
